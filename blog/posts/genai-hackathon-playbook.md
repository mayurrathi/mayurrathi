# The GenAI Hackathon Playbook: Lessons from 1,100+ Participants

Running a hackathon for 1,100+ participants across multiple geographies sounds overwhelming. Here's how we made it work — and produced 24 client-ready GenAI use cases.

## Context

At Capgemini, I was tasked with organizing a global GenAI hackathon to:
- Upskill engineers on generative AI technologies
- Produce practical use cases that could be pitched to clients
- Build internal excitement around our AI capabilities

## Phase 1: Pre-Hackathon (6 Weeks Out)

### Building the Foundation

Before anyone writes a line of code, you need three things:

1. **Clear problem statements** — We worked with 8 business units to define real client challenges
2. **Learning paths** — Created a 2-week "GenAI Bootcamp" with curated resources
3. **Team formation** — Encouraged cross-functional teams (dev + domain + design)

### The Bootcamp Approach

Instead of throwing participants into the deep end, we ran a structured learning path:

- **Week 1**: Fundamentals of LLMs, prompt engineering, and RAG architectures
- **Week 2**: Hands-on labs with Google Cloud Vertex AI and open-source models

This pre-work was critical. Teams that completed the bootcamp were **3x more likely** to submit a viable solution.

## Phase 2: The Hackathon (48 Hours)

### Structure That Scales

- **8 tracks** aligned to business domains (Healthcare, Finance, Manufacturing, etc.)
- **Dedicated mentors** for each track (senior architects who volunteered)
- **Checkpoint presentations** at 12h and 24h to prevent teams from going off-track
- **Slack channels** for real-time support

### Tools & Infrastructure

Every team got:
- Google Cloud credits for Vertex AI
- Pre-configured development environments
- Template repositories with starter code
- Access to enterprise APIs for testing

## Phase 3: Post-Hackathon

The hackathon doesn't end when submissions close. This is where the real value begins:

1. **Judging panel** with client-facing leadership
2. **Top 24 solutions** refined into proper proposals
3. **3 solutions** went into active client engagements
4. **All code** open-sourced internally for reuse

## Key Takeaways

- **Pre-work is everything.** Don't expect innovation from people who are still learning the basics.
- **Mentors > prizes.** Good mentorship produces better outcomes than bigger prize pools.
- **Think beyond the event.** The post-hackathon pipeline is where business value is created.
- **Measure real impact.** Our success metric wasn't "projects submitted" — it was "solutions deployed to clients."
